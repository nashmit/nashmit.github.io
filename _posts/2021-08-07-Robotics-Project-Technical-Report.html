AbstractThis report presents three different ways to imple-
ment Inverse Kinematics that is applicable in a context of a
serial robot manipulator and the limitations that each of them
has. The solutions chosen are based on Numerical Jacobian,
Denavit-Hartenberg Algorithmic Jacobian, FABRIK and Cyclic
Coordinate Descent.

I. INTRODUCTION
The main scope of this Robotics Practical project is the
analysis and implementation of different approaches to solve
the Inverse Kinematics problem for an open loop chain
robotic arm.
Inverse Kinematics (IK) is dened as the problem
of determining a set of appropriate joint congurations for
which the end-effectors move to desired positions as smoothly,
rapidly, and as accurately as possible. This problem nds
its application in the area of robotics, computer animation,
ergonomics and the computer games industry.
During the last decades, several methods and techniques,
sophisticated or heuristic, have been presented to produce fast
and realistic solutions to the IK problem. However, most of
the currently available methods suffer from high computa-
tional cost, production of unrealistic poses and/or numerical
instability. The aim of this report is to present in full the
most popular approaches and to underline the strength and
weaknesses of each of them. Moreover, Denavit-Hartenberg
Algorithmic Jacobian will be introduced as an intermediate
step for computing Inverse Kinematics [3].

Limitations
Two major limitations of this approach became apparent, when
we tried to apply it to the KUKA robot: In its original form,
FABRIK is designed to be applied on a 2D plane, and it,
moreover, presumes that the joints can be rotated around
any rotation axis. The KUKA robot, however, is a spatial
mechanism and consists solely of hinge joints, which only
allow rotation along a single rotation axis.
The creators of the algorithm have proposed an extension
for the algorithm that allows spatial movements and respects
hinge joint constraints [4]. Unfortunately, it turned out that it
is not possible to apply this extension to our KUKA robot
as it requires the root joint to be able to move freely without
any restrictions [11].
Furthermore, the algorithm does not work, if the target point
lies on a chain robot whose joints are all located on a straight
line [2], e.g., had we decreased the radius of the circle along
which the planar chain robot should move when we were
testing our implementation, the system would have stayed put.
Conclusion
In its original form, FABRIK is simple to implement,
computationally inexpensive, and, as it is an iterative
approach, produces visually smooth, natural-looking
movements [3]. It always converges as long as the target is
within reach [2], i.e., the distance between the end-effector
and target point is smaller or equal to the sum of the link
lengths l
i
, and it can be extended to allow spherical joint
limits and multiple end-effectors [2], [4].
Due to its heuristic nature and depending on the initial con-
guration and target point, it might take a lot of iterations to
reach the exact target position. Its biggest drawback, however,
is that it cannot be applied on a kinematic chain which only
consists of hinges like the KUKA robot. Thus, we could not
use it for this project, and have, therefore, examined another
heuristic algorithm which will be introduced in the following
subsection.

Additionally, we have to keep in mind that when we use the
dot product to calculate the angle between the rotation axes
h and r (see Figure 7) for each joint, there are two possible
scenarios: The angle between them is smaller than 90, which
illustratively speaking means that the vectors lie on the same
side of the plane. In this case h is closer to the positive r
than to the negative r and we can simply use the angle
with a positive sign to carry on with the algorithm. If the
angle is bigger than 90, the two vectors do not lie on the
same side of the plane. Hence, h is closer to the negative
r, which corresponds to a rotation around r at the angle
with a negative sign. In our implementation, we have taken
this into account, by calculating the difference between the
end-effector and target point after rotating at the positive and
negative angle, respectively, and then choosing the angle that
yields the smaller distance.
Finally, if the resultant rotation leads to the end-effector
moving further away from the target point (which we realised
can happen if we include hinge constrains, see previous
section), we discard the result.
In theory, it is easy to add the limits in the ranges of
motion of the joints to the algorithm, which is necessary to
produce movements that the real KUKA robot can actually
perform. One simply has to clip the resultant angles to the
maximum or minimum angle, if they exceed the maximum
or fall below the minimum angle that the joint can rotate to,
respectively [12]. However, since in this project the initial
conguration of the real robot and the model in the .lua les
differ from each other, the limits of the rotation angles of
the joints that can be taken from the robots manual [10], do
not correspond to the limits of the angles of the .lua model.
Thus, we did not add this detail to our implementation.

Limitations
One of the biggest drawbacks of this approach is that, depend-
ing on the starting conguration of the robot and the target
point, we can run into local minima.

[5], [8].
If the rotation axis r and the hinge axis h of the joint
(see Figure 7) are perpendicular to each other, rotating
around r will have no effect on h, which means that
the joint is not moved. If this happens for all the joints
in a robot, none of them will be able to move, thus it
cannot get closer to the target, and, since the algorithm
does not allow rotations that move the end-effector further
away from the target position, it is stuck in the local minimum.
Conclusion
Like the FABRIK algorithm, CCD is easy to implement,
computationally inexpensive [3] and does not suffer from
singularities. For our project, the most important difference
between the two was that CCD can be modied to work for
systems with hinge joints, so that we were able to apply it to
the KUKA robot.

C. Outlook for the heuristic approaches
In future projects, the two heuristic approaches could be
improved by trying to eliminate their disadvantages.
Recently, a paper proposing a modication to the FABRIK
algorithm called FABRIK-R has been published [11]. It
makes adding hinge joint constraints to FABRIK possible,
without requiring the root joint to be able to move freely,
which would allow us to apply FABRIK to the KUKA robot.
It would be interesting to implement this modication and
compare its performance and results to the CCD algorithm.
To tackle CCDs problem of running into local minima,
one could implement a routine that detects local minima,
i.e., that notices if the distance between the end-effector and
target point stopped decreasing, even though the system is
not close enough to the target yet. Once a local minimum
has been detected, the joints are distorted slightly by a
random value, such that the rotation of axes of the hinge
and the calculated rotation, are not perpendicular to each other.
Both algorithms would benet from having the joints co-
operate to reach the target position and thus make the joints
aware of the other joints in the hierarchy [9]. Currently,
solutions in which a joint is rst moved away from the target
position, so that its parent joint can move it closer to the target
in the next step of the algorithm (see Figure 8), are discarded.

C. Computing the Jacobian
The Jacobian can be computed in three ways: symbolically,
numerically, or algorithmically. Both the symbolical and
numerical approaches suffer from artifacts.
On the one hand, nite differences are expensive, since you
need to do a forward pass for each derivative. It also induces
huge numerical artifacts mostly because of the non-linearity
from the hierarchy which can end with an unstable numerical
algorithm. On the other hand, symbolic differentiation can
result in complex and redundant expressions which can also
end up with fast error propagation.
The third approach is more related to Automatic
Differentiation (AD) as it is both efcient and numerically
stable. The goal of this approach is not to nd a formula but
a general procedure to compute the derivative. The difference
from AD is that it is applied specically in the context of
I.K. where we have open chains.
For the Jacobian-based I.K. we will be focusing on the third
approach.
D. Denavit-Hartenberg Algorithmic Jacobian
In order to make the Jacobian computational numerically
stable and easy to scale we have chosen to represent the
homogeneous chain of transformations in independent 1 DOF

E. Hinge constrains and extra constrains
The algorithm incorporates the hinge constraints by
construction, based on the process of assigning the Denavit-
Hartenberg reference frames.
The process of adding new position and orientation con-
straints w.r.t. other points from the hierarchy is easily achiev-
able by executing inverse kinematics at the same time for
multiple points. This step was not fully implemented but
the framework implementation was done by taking this into
consideration. The only thing missing from the current imple-
mentation is a consistent interlace.
It is also worth mentioning that the implemented solution is
not limited to the KUKA robot but can be used on any dened
open-chain robot by building new links and interconnecting
them with joins in a general way.
F. Implementation and testing
The development process was done by continuously build-
ing tests as there was no way to check the validity of the
results, since the hierarchy and the sizes from RBDL did
unfortunately not match. All of them can be found in the
tests folder.
Finally, to check the validity of the solution a second
implementation, using the numerical Jacobian, was done as
this one is easier to implement and less prone to errors.
For checking the validity, one can use the IK Test cmake
target. Multiple targets are added there ( position and orien-
tation ). For switching between the numerical approach and
D-H approach, one can comment the corresponding line from
KuKa KR5 R850 D H.cpp ( line 241/242 ).
A video le was also added in which both methods can be
seen together: D-H Jacobian vs Numerical Jacobian
Comprehensive testing was not done but a few observations
were possible. By comparing the two approaches we were able
to observe, in a consistent way, that the D-H Jacobian is faster
and more stable. From time to time, the numerical solution was
not able to nd feasible solutions due to numerical errors.
G. Limitation
The default algorithm does not work with closed loops but
it can be adapted.
An important limitation of the algorithm is the possibility to
enter into singularities. This can happen as a result of Gimbal
lock. Different avoiding approaches can be considered. The
most notorious is based on the penalization of paths that can
go too close to singularities. Another workaround can be to
switch to a geometrical approach whenever one ends up near
a singularity and to return when it is not.
H. Outlook of the Inverse Kinematics algorithms
Besides the three methods presented in this report, other
methods exist. In the class of Jacobian methods, we can
mention SVD, Damped Least Square, Pseudo-inverse Damped
Least Squared. There are also second-order methods ( based on
Newton Method ) [3]. Most of them have similar properties
and are used extensively in robotics as they can easily be
integrated into multibody dynamics as well as kinematics. This
is not the case for heuristic methods.
Some mixed approaches have been tested, in which CCD is
used in the beginning for its fast convergence and the process
gets nished using a Jacobian method. This approach does not
always work well as the solutions of the heuristic methods
tend to look unnatural. This is due to the way the process
works, e.g., you might end up with a solution just by moving
the rst link when in fact one might be interested in a more
global solution. For that matter, some regularization can be
introduced. Unfortunately, this implies manually tuning which
does not always scale well.
